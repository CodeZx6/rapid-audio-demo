RAPID: Reliable Acoustic Priors for Efficient Inference in Diffusion-based Speech Enhancement

Anonymous authors
Under double blind review

Abstract

Diffusion based speech enhancement restores plausible clean speech by iteratively applying a learned speech prior. However, reverse sampling is computationally expensive, and the learned prior remains tied to training acoustics that may not match real world conditions. We address both challenges by exploiting acoustic structure that can be reliably measured from each noisy mixture to adapt the sampler to the real-time scene and reduce the burden on learned generation. This motivates a hierarchy of noisy speech in which the noise floor and articulation rhythm serve as physical priors, while ambiguous fine speech detail remains the responsibility of the learned prior. Guided by this hierarchy, we propose RAPID, a training free framework that uses reliable acoustic priors to improve inference efficiency in frozen diffusion and flow based models. RAPID uses these physical priors to correct the conditioning mixture and anchor the sampler starting state, while constrained step optimization selects the smallest validated number of function evaluations (NFE) that preserves enhancement quality. To evaluate robustness under distinct shifts, we further construct two reproducible datasets that probe changes in noise family and speech style under a shared mixing protocol. Across four backbones and five acoustic conditions, RAPID consistently improves perceptual quality while reducing NFE by factors of 1.25× to 8.3×. Beyond these datasets, we integrate RAPID directly into the audio pipeline of an Ameca humanoid without robot specific training or sampler modification, demonstrating practical inference efficiency and deployment compatibility under real acoustic conditions.

Method

Live on a humanoid head

Noisy utterances were played into a real room and picked up by the ear microphones of an Ameca humanoid head. Each captured utterance was enhanced on a remote GPU, and the enhanced speech was played back through the robot’s chest speaker. The nine utterances below come from a single continuous take: four passes over the same nine sentences, each backbone at its default number of function evaluations (NFE) and the same frozen checkpoint with +RAPID at a reduced NFE. Nothing is cut — the wait you see is the wait that happened in the room.

Every trial has the same four stages: the robot hears the utterance, the frozen sampler runs its inference, the enhanced audio is pushed across the network into the robot’s playback pipeline, and the robot repeats it. All four timelines in a row are drawn on one shared axis, so the stages line up and can be read off against each other: hearing and repeat are identical by construction, push is a fixed cost of a few tenths of a second, and inference is the only stage +RAPID touches. Under each video you can also listen to the two ends of the pipeline on their own — what reached the microphones, and the enhanced speech that came back.

The timer starts the moment the speaker falls silent and stops when the robot’s own speaker begins to sound, so it carries the sampler, the network hop and the playback pipeline together. It is read off the recording’s audio track and agrees with the sampler’s logged inference time to within 0.1–0.6 s, which is exactly the push. Over the nine utterances the Schrödinger bridge answers in 1.68 s with +RAPID against 4.93 s at its default, and SGMSE+ in 2.73 s against 5.78 s. In 3 of the 36 trials a logging fault truncated the archived copy of the enhanced speech; those players carry the offline re-run of the same enhancer on the same input, and the audio heard in the video is untouched.
